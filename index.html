About

From keeping clusters up to keeping models honest

I didn't start out in AI. My first two years out of my undergrad degree were spent as a Kubernetes Architect at Atos-Syntel, on a small team responsible for keeping production clusters alive: renewing certificates before they expired, scaling node pools before traffic spiked, and staying calm when pods started failing at 2am. That grounding taught me something most AI courses don't: a system is only as good as its worst day in production.

That's the lens I carried into my Master of Artificial Intelligence at RMIT. I went in curious about models and came out even more interested in the systems that carry them into production. That shift became concrete during my internship at Prodegee, where I worked on a geospatial classification pipeline that went well beyond a notebook full of experiments. It ran through a proper GitHub kanban workflow, went through peer review, and handed off to Kestra for orchestration, with the output consumed by two other teams downstream. Watching a model I built get used by people I'd never spoken to made the "production" part of production ML click in a way no lecture ever had.

These days I'm drawn to roles that sit at that intersection: AI Solutions Architect, Agentic AI Engineer, and similar titles that care as much about how an AI system is designed, deployed, and kept running as about the model inside it. If a role needs someone who can talk infrastructure and machine learning in the same sentence, that's usually where I add the most value.

Outside of work, I'm the person who still reads the postmortem after something breaks, even when it isn't my system to fix — curiosity that translates directly into how I approach a new codebase or pipeline.

Experience

Where I've worked

Two roles, one thread. First keeping infrastructure reliable, now keeping ML pipelines reliable too.

AI/ML Engineering Intern
Prodegee (via RMIT)
Feb 2026 - Jun 2026

I joined Prodegee to get hands-on experience with ML the way real teams build it: version-controlled, peer-reviewed, and reproducible, rather than a one-off notebook. The project was a geospatial classification pipeline used to predict outcomes across villages in Mauritania from survey and geospatial data.

  • Worked inside an agentic, version-controlled pipeline: every task moved through a GitHub kanban board with a documented kickoff and a peer review before it was considered done, and Kestra handled orchestrating the pipeline steps end to end.
  • Built an XGBoost classifier on an 8-country geospatial corpus and ran 100 Optuna trials to tune it, lifting the model's F1 score in the process.
  • Benchmarked XGBoost against Random Forest and Logistic Regression across 200 surveyed villages, then tested generalization on 35 additional villages that had no survey data at all.
  • Tracked down a feature collapse bug that was quietly wrecking model quality. Fixing it took the key feature-target correlation from -0.714 to +0.600, which is the difference between a model that's actively misleading and one that's usable.
  • Built an out-of-distribution flagging system so the model could be trusted to run blind inference on 2,996 villages that had never been surveyed, instead of only the ones with labeled data.
  • Validated the final predictions visually in QGIS before handing off production-ready JSON artifacts to two downstream teams (DS2 and DA1) who built on top of the output.
Kubernetes Architect
Atos-Syntel
Sep 2021 - Jun 2023

My first role out of my Bachelor's, on a 7-person engineering team supporting production Kubernetes infrastructure for a US-based client (under NDA, so I can't name them). This is where I learned to think in terms of uptime, blast radius, and what happens when something breaks and people are depending on it staying up.

  • Upgraded production Kubernetes clusters on a regular cycle, planning each upgrade carefully so the cluster stayed current without breaking workloads that were already running.
  • Renewed TLS certificates for the clusters on schedule, the kind of unglamorous work that only gets noticed when it's missed.
  • Scaled nodes up and down in response to workload demand, balancing cost against having enough headroom for traffic spikes.
  • Built working knowledge of the core services that keep a cluster running (Ingress, Pods, kube-scheduler, kube-controller-manager, kube-proxy), which mattered most when something broke and I needed to know where to start looking.
  • Troubleshot incidents on critical services like Pods and Ingress, digging through logs and cluster events to find root causes under time pressure.
  • Monitored cluster health continuously using Dynatrace, which caught a fair number of problems before they turned into incidents.

Working inside a 7-person team on one client for nearly two years also taught me the less technical half of the job: staying easy to work with, picking things up quickly when the client's setup didn't match anything I'd seen before, and being someone teammates could hand a problem to without having to double check it afterward.

Cloud / Backend

Music Subscription App

Problem
Most tutorials show you one way to deploy an app on AWS. I wanted to actually understand the trade-offs between the options, not just take someone's word for which one is "best."
Approach
Built one Flask-based music subscription service, then deployed the same application three different ways: on EC2, on ECS Fargate, and as Lambda functions, each backed by DynamoDB and S3, and containerized with Docker where it made sense.
Outcome
A working, hands-on comparison of cost, latency, and operational overhead across a traditional VM, a managed container service, and a fully serverless approach, instead of a purely theoretical answer.
